Section 01

Mathematical Foundation

Fractional Differencing. Raw prices are non-stationary, trend indefinitely and violate the assumptions most models require. Integer differencing (d=1) fixes this but destroys all serial correlation. Fractional differencing at d=0.4 finds the minimum differentiation that enforces stationarity while retaining the long-memory signal that drives momentum.

Integer (d=1) enforces stationarity, destroys memory
yt = xt − xt−1 = (1 − L) xt
Fractional (d=0.4) enforces stationarity, preserves memory
yt = (1−L)d xt = xt − d·xt−1 + d(d−1)/2!·xt−2 − ···

Hurst Exponent. H characterises long-range dependence via rescaled range analysis, a model-free way to classify whether a market is currently trending or mean-reverting. This is fundamentally different from any technical indicator: it measures the geometry of price paths, not their level or rate of change.

Rescaled range, basis of Hurst estimation
E[R(n) / S(n)] = C · nH
Hurst Exponent, Regime Classification

Rolling Hurst is computed over two windows (100d, 150d). Their difference H_delta captures regime transitions, often the highest-signal moment when the market shifts character.

Section 05

Feature Interpretation (SHAP)

SHAP attributes each prediction to individual features in a game-theoretically fair way. The top-ranked features are the interaction terms confirming that regime-conditional signal encoding adds genuine predictive value beyond the features in isolation.

The model learned without being explicitly told that RSI means different things in trending vs mean-reverting markets. This is precisely what the Hurst × RSI interaction term encodes, and why it ranks as the single most important feature.
